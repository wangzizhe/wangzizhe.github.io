From I- to T- to π-shaped Talent: The Evolution of Talent Structures in the AI Era

Looking back, the most valued type of talent used to be I-shaped talent, people who deeply specialized in a single industry or professional field and built strong expertise there. In the industrial era, this model worked extremely well because labor was clearly divided. Each person only needed to focus on a small part of the work and do it extremely well. But today, having deep expertise in just one field is no longer enough. What is increasingly needed now is T-shaped talent: people who have deep expertise in one domain while also possessing knowledge and awareness across multiple disciplines. For example, in modern software development, compared with the highly specialized division of roles in the past, developers today are increasingly expected not only to write code but also to understand product logic, business requirements, and even user experience. A great engineer today is not only a developer but also someone with a certain level of product thinking.

The development of AI technology has followed a similar path. After the breakthrough of deep learning around 2012, most AI systems relied on specialized models. Different domains usually had their own models and solutions. For example, computer vision had its own model architectures, while natural language processing relied on entirely different structures. This changed after the rise of large language models in 2022. People gradually realized that the Transformer architecture could unify many different tasks. At the time, many entrepreneurs were asking an important question: should we build specialized models or general-purpose models? Over time, the answer became clear. General-purpose models became the dominant direction. By 2025, we had entered the era of AI Agents. The success of general AI Agents such as Manus and OpenClaw showed a new possibility. AI is no longer just a model, but an intelligent system capable of executing complex tasks. From an entrepreneurial perspective, I believe AI Agents represent one of the most important opportunities today.

However, the direction I am most optimistic about is not general-purpose agents but domain-specific AI Agents. The reason is simple. General large models already have a reasonably good basic understanding of the world, but once problems move into specific industries, their capabilities often decline significantly. This is because much of the core knowledge in many industries does not exist in publicly available internet text. Instead, it comes from years of real-world practice and accumulated experience. As a result, after the success of general AI Agents, it is very likely that we will see a wave of domain-specific AI Agents.

Behind this lies a very clear talent gap. On one side, the people building general large models usually do not have deep knowledge of specific industries because their focus is on general capabilities. On the other side, many industry experts have years of experience but often lack the motivation or ability to study AI technologies in depth. This creates a rare type of talent: people who both possess deep domain expertise and understand technologies such as deep learning, large language models, and AI Agents. Such people are extremely rare, but they are exactly the ones most likely to create truly valuable domain-specific AI Agents. This is also the main direction of my current entrepreneurial efforts. I also encourage people to think about a simple question within the industries they know well: can AI Agents reshape certain key parts of that industry?

At the same time, AI is also changing how individuals develop their abilities. As AI-assisted tools become increasingly powerful, the cost of learning new knowledge is rapidly decreasing. People can now learn new fields much faster than before. In this environment, we may begin to see a new type of talent structure: π-shaped talent. In other words, instead of having deep expertise in just one field, a person may develop deep knowledge and experience in multiple fields. When expertise from different domains intersects within the same person, it becomes much easier to create entirely new product forms and business models. We often talk about the multimodal capabilities of AI, such as text, images, video, and audio. In the future, the multimodal capabilities of humans will be just as important, meaning the ability to combine knowledge, experience, and ways of thinking from different disciplines.

Over the years, I have come to believe that people should try more things and accumulate more experiences. We should not quickly assume that something is useless. As long as we take things seriously, many experiences that once seemed unrelated will eventually connect. When those experiences finally connect, you may suddenly realize that you have become increasingly unique. Looking back now, I realize that many of the things I did in the past were quietly building the capabilities I rely on today, and very little effort is truly wasted.

Therefore, in today's world, we should at least strive to become T-shaped talents: building deep expertise in one field while maintaining awareness across other domains. In the future, we may even have the opportunity to become π-shaped talents: developing deep expertise in multiple fields and connecting them together. In the age of AI, such people will become increasingly valuable.
