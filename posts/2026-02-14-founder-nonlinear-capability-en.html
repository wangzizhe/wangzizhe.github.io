The Founder's Nonlinear Capability

In the context of startups and investing, the idea of non-linear ability is often mentioned. At its core, it is an understanding of leverage, uncertainty, and how industries and technologies amplify one another. Many startup failures are not caused by a lack of capability, but by the fact that the effort invested from the very beginning sits in a region that cannot be amplified.

What most people are actually good at are linear skills. These include converting effort into output under stable rules, refining processes, and optimizing within clear constraints. Such skills are extremely valuable in mature systems such as large companies, established industries, and well-defined paths. But the early stage of a startup is not a stable system. It is highly uncertain and strongly path-dependent. In this environment, many things do not scale proportionally with effort, even when executed perfectly. Many failures are not due to insufficient effort, but because the effort itself carries little leverage.

To me, non-linear ability is not abstract. It shows up repeatedly in how decisions are made. Its starting point is not “Can I do this well?” but rather “If this works, will the outcome be amplified exponentially?” I shifted from mechanical and automobile engineering to deep learning in 2019 primarily because I believed the automotive industry would be restructured by software and AI, and that many other industries would be systematically transformed by AI as well. This was not a gradual optimization, but a directional choice. Had I not adjusted my trajectory in time, I would likely have missed a decisive technological window. Many non-linear differences come from choices that appear quiet at the time, but have disproportionately large consequences.

Founders do not win by making more decisions. They win by making decisions that can be amplified at a small number of critical moments. These include choosing a domain rather than polishing features, selecting an entry point rather than expanding boundaries, and deciding whether to continue validation or walk away. I felt this strongly during my doctoral research as well. What truly matters is understanding the research landscape early on, knowing where the state of the art lies, and only then identifying which research gaps are genuinely worth pursuing. If the direction is wrong, even high-quality execution later on produces very limited marginal returns.

When a system can only grow with continuous attention from the founder, it is still fundamentally linear.

This is an easily overlooked reality. A 10% improvement in technology does not translate into a 10% improvement in business outcomes. More complex models do not automatically create stronger moats. Harder problems are not necessarily more valuable. Many highly intelligent and hardworking people do not fail because of insufficient ability, but because they consistently work on problems that are only linearly improvable. In the early stages of a startup, what is truly scarce is not doing things better, but doing things that can be amplified.

For me, entrepreneurship is a brutal but highly efficient training ground. It simultaneously exposes uncertainty, real-world feedback, long-term games, and the complexity of organizations and people. What I am working on now is essentially an experiment. The question is whether I can solidify personal judgment, technical understanding, and abstraction ability into a system that can be repeatedly validated and amplified by the market.

Non-linear ability is not a persona, nor is it a declaration of ambition. It functions more like a filter. If an endeavor has uncontrollable downside, limited upside amplification, and little transferable learning, it is unlikely to be a good non-linear training ground. To me, the meaning of building a startup is not guaranteed success. It lies in whether I consistently place myself at points of high-leverage decision-making, and whether I am accumulating a capability structure that gives me sustained optionality in the future.
