A Long Talk in the Snow

This Tuesday, I attended YETI's Demo Day. YETI is an incubator where different teams present their startup projects. The event itself was interesting, but the most interesting part actually happened after it ended.

On the way out, I was walking with a German guy I had met there. We chatted briefly near the exit, said goodbye outside, and then started talking again because a few more questions came up. It was snowing, and the conversation kept going deeper. We got so engaged that we stood there in the snow for more than half an hour. Our clothes were soaked, but neither of us noticed while talking.

I've been in Germany for quite a while now. This guy is one of the very few Germans I know who is genuinely interested in how the world is changing and whose understanding keeps evolving with it. To be blunt, many people I've met in Germany, young or old, still seem to understand today's world through an earlier lens, unless their work is directly connected to China or the U.S., or they have spent substantial time living in one of those countries. It is not that they lack knowledge, but that the worldview they rely on often lags noticeably behind the reality unfolding today. If one mainly relies on the news to understand the world, it is very easy to remain at the level of narratives, while missing the deeper changes that have already taken place.

What surprised me was that this guy has never been to China or the U.S. and has lived in Dresden all along, yet his understanding of the world is remarkably current. Our views on technology and the future were highly aligned. In Germany, it is rare for me to find people willing to seriously discuss how the world is changing and where it might be heading. On the one hand, many people's mental models are still rooted in the past. On the other hand, one pattern I keep noticing is that many people are not genuinely interested in how the world itself is changing.

On some level, today's Germany reminds me of late-Qing China: it still stands on the order, institutions, and industrial prestige accumulated in the past, shows limited interest in external change and new things, remains immersed in its own system, and underestimates the structural transformations already taking place in the outside world. If it cannot renew itself in time, Germany may well drift into a similar trajectory of decline. Humanity keeps moving forward, but many historical patterns tend to return in new forms.

Look at Germany’s job market today. From my own observation, the hiring landscape has not changed much compared with ten years ago. It is hard to find roles focused on autonomous driving, in-vehicle systems, large language models, or embodied AI, and even deep learning roles are rare. These industries still have not reached meaningful scale in Germany and Europe as a whole. If placed in the broader framework of global tech competition, the compensation levels, growth opportunities, and career ceilings offered by leading German and European companies are, on the whole, difficult to compare with those of first-tier tech ecosystems in China and the U.S. Over the past twenty years, Germany has not occupied a sufficiently central position in several of the key technology waves that have defined the new era. It still lacks a new generation of globally influential tech companies (SAP is more enterprise software and Siemens is more industrial systems, and neither represents the kind of company leading today’s technology wave). As a result, I often feel a kind of dissonance: in many ways, today’s Germany feels almost unchanged from the last century. In the last century, Germany was an undisputed world power. Today, the situation is entirely different, because the world has continued moving forward while Germany has largely remained where it was. More importantly, Germany’s foundational industries are now under pressure: manufacturing and the automotive sector have entered a structural downturn, old industries are declining, new ones are failing to take off, and politicians keep repeating slogans. Since 2024, I have stopped believing any political slogans. I have come to feel more and more strongly that many politicians are not truly prioritizing long-term national competitiveness, but rather short-term political gains and their own positions. More realistically, many issues remain stuck in discussion for years, and by the time a real decision is finally made, the window of opportunity for the relevant industry has often already closed. And among many of the people I meet, I often sense a kind of inward retreat: they care more about preserving the small, familiar world around them than about actively engaging with where their country is heading, and they seem to lack the drive to change the status quo. That drive, by contrast, is something I often feel among young people in China. Without enough jobs and without a strong enough tax base, Germany’s current social system will sooner or later face greater pressure. For now, it can still rely on the foundations accumulated in the past, but I do not think this window will last much longer. Over the next decade, Germany will very likely enter a period of far greater structural strain. I am not German, but I still feel a deep sense of regret about Germany’s present and future.

So meeting this German young man was a real bright moment for me. We went deep on topics that are usually hard to discuss here. Before we parted, I told him: you have to keep going and try to help rebuild the future of Germany and Europe!

If I were German, I would most likely make this my mission. I genuinely like this country, and I like the people here as well. Since arriving in Germany, I have tried to approach everything with a learning mindset, and to view this place with humility, openness, and tolerance. But there is a reality that cannot be avoided: Germany and Europe are indeed facing very serious problems today. What shocks me even more is that many Germans and Europeans do not seem to have fully realized the seriousness of these problems; and even when they do, there still does not seem to be enough momentum to push for real change. Especially over the past two years, as I have looked more deeply into how Germany has gradually fallen out of sync with the world and has been steadily pushed aside in the new round of competition, I have become increasingly certain of one thing: I still have choices. Either I find a unique and critical field in which I can create genuinely scarce value in Germany and Europe, or I go to a place with stronger growth momentum, greater density of opportunity, and a better environment for realizing my ideals.
