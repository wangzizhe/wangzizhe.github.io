When Everything Can Be Replaced by AI, What Will Be Left of Us?

Since last year, I’ve been thinking about one question over and over again: in the age of AI agents, what do humans still have that cannot be replaced? Many people feel deep anxiety about AI, and that anxiety is not groundless. AI agents are replacing large amounts of labor at an extremely fast pace, and unlike previous technological revolutions, there is currently no clear pattern where a disappearing job is matched by the creation of a new one. When a human role is replaced, it is often simply gone rather than transformed into another position. Recently, some people have even argued that the entire software and SaaS industries may face structural disruption. I largely agree with these assessments.

But people who know me well also know this: I am genuinely excited about the arrival of the AI agent era. So far in my life, no technological wave has ever made me feel this excited. And so the question becomes more concrete. In a highly automated future that I myself can hardly fully imagine, what exactly will remain that AI cannot replace?

To answer this question, I realized I had to return to something more fundamental: why am I alive in the first place? A few years ago, I read The Selfish Gene by Richard Dawkins, and it left a deep impression on me. The book presents a highly counterintuitive yet internally consistent idea. It is not species that evolve in order to survive, but genes that use organisms in order to replicate themselves. From this perspective, living beings, including humans, are merely vehicles for genes to survive and reproduce. Many instincts we take for granted, such as the desire to survive, sexual drive, hunger, thirst, and sleepiness, are essentially traits selected by natural selection because they help genes persist. I found this idea fascinating.

Starting last year, I also began training seriously in the gym. Because my approach was relatively correct, my strength increased in a fairly linear way. After a little more than half a year, my 5RM bench press reached 72.5 kg, and my 5RM Romanian deadlift reached 107.5 kg. During this process, I gradually realized something important. Strength gains do not primarily come from muscle growth itself, but from improvements in neural recruitment. Put more plainly, the brain and nervous system are learning how to control the body more effectively and release more energy. This made me even more aware that most of our behaviors and abilities in daily life are fundamentally the result of interactions between DNA and brain function.

What makes humans truly remarkable, however, is that we possess self-awareness. Humans are among the few species capable of resisting their own genes. We can delay gratification, sacrifice short-term pleasure for long-term goals, and deliberately train ourselves to unlock and expand the brain’s control over the body. One of the meanings of being alive, then, is not merely to function as a tool for gene replication, but to exist as a conscious being. DNA, the brain, and consciousness together shape a complete human individual.

Biological life itself is also profoundly unique. Sometimes when I look at my cat, I can’t help wondering why cats possess so many “superpowers.” Their ears can rotate freely, their bodies are incredibly agile, their night vision is exceptional, and their spines are extremely flexible. And yet at the same time, they can seem a bit silly. A laser pointer or a cat teaser toy is enough to completely override whatever they were doing before. Over time, I realized that cats are close to a kind of ultimate form along the path of biological evolution. Their body size, muscles, reflexes, and senses are all optimized to serve the role of a solitary hunter. Their structure is minimal yet highly efficient, and despite remaining largely unchanged for tens of millions of years, they continue to thrive in a wide range of environments. This made the wonder of nature feel strikingly tangible to me.

When we turn back to humans, although we lack many of the seemingly terrifying abilities cats have, humans have evolved a set of abilities that are equally rare, and in some cases extreme, in the natural world. These include extraordinary long-distance endurance running, the ability to repeatedly output high levels of power, a remarkable capacity for motor learning and imitation, and a willingness to push ourselves deliberately to our limits. These abilities are so commonplace in everyday life that we rarely recognize how unusual they are. It is only when I truly stop and think about them that I realize how extraordinary they actually are.

For me, a large part of the meaning of being alive lies in feeling and experiencing. In the age of AI agents, as more and more tasks are delegated to agents, I find myself with more time and energy to think, to feel, and to experience. My sensitivity to experience seems to have been amplified, and I have become more consciously aware of each moment. For example, sitting on the couch or lying in bed, holding my girlfriend with my left arm or being held by her, while my right hand strokes our clingy cat, a creature that feels both fully evolved and endlessly affectionate. I believe this kind of experience is something AI can never truly possess, because it belongs to carbon-based life, and especially to humans.

Equally important is experience in the sense of lived history. Humans choose, endure, and live through both success and failure, joy and pain. These emotions and experiences are among our most precious assets. I often think back to my early twenties now. The relationships I had then still feel like some of the most beautiful experiences of my life, and the intense pain of breakups felt unbearable at the time. In hindsight, however, those moments are just as valuable. They shaped who I am today. I also know that ten years from now, I will frequently think back to this very period of my life. Every present moment becomes a future memory that can never be replicated. That is why the present is always worth cherishing.

I believe these abilities are things AI can never truly acquire. Just as different species among carbon-based life have evolved different strengths, silicon-based life will also have its own advantages and limitations. AI will reach extremes in the areas it excels at, but the human capacity for feeling and experience remains uniquely human. Everything we have lived through, all our joy and sorrow, shape individuals who cannot be copied. Humans go through life without taking much with them in the end, but we do not need to take anything with us. These feelings and experiences themselves are our most distinctive form of value.

For this reason, I now devote much of my time to thinking, creating, feeling, and experiencing. I believe that in this age, one of the most important human abilities is creativity, because the cost of executing ideas is constantly decreasing, while the ability to clearly understand what is worth doing is becoming increasingly rare. By handing more tasks over to AI, I have freed myself to focus on what feels more essential. The most interesting part is that many of my best ideas do not emerge while I am actively trying to think, but rather when my brain is at rest. For me, the most effective way to generate creative ideas is to think deeply about something first and then go do something else. Many ideas emerge unexpectedly while walking, commuting, or training.

Whether we are talking about different species, carbon-based life versus silicon-based life, humanity as a whole, or each individual person, every existence is unique. In the age of AI agents, what we should truly do is not to be consumed by anxiety about replacement, but to liberate ourselves and devote more time to thinking, creating, feeling, and experiencing.

To take what it means to be human, and push it as far as possible.
